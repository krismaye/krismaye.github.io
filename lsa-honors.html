SI 622: Needs Assessment and Usability Evaluation

SI 622 covers the key concepts of evaluation, and a variety of methods used to determine the goals of a system or service: performing organizational analysis; assessing task/technology or service fit; determining ease of learning a new or existing system/service; determining ease of use; assessing aspects of performance; and evaluating system success in accomplishing the user/organizational goals. Methods include observation, surveys, interviews, heuristic evaluation, and usability tests.

About the Client

The LSA Honors program is a department within the College of Literature, Science and the Arts at the University of Michigan. This department maintains the LSA Honors website, which contains information for prospective students, current students, faculty and staff, and alumni and friends. Because they are a department within LSA, the Honors Program is required to use the Adobe Experience Manager (AEM) content management system, in addition to using the standard website template for all LSA departments. This poses some difficulties because it means the Honors Program has limited ability to make changes to their website.

Findings

One of the main concerns our stakeholders had about the LSA Honors website is that it is hard for users to find the information they are looking for. After learning this, we determined that we should focus our research on the information architecture and information hierarchy present within the LSA Honors website.

In addition to our stakeholders, we also interviewed five current LSA Honors students to get their thoughts about the website. Based on these interviews, we learned these students did have difficulty finding information, mainly due to text-heavy pages and unclear navigation. We used the demographic information and opinions of our interviewees to create three personas for the LSA Honors website: Josh, a not particularily tech-savvy junior; Melanie, a sophomore who is very involved in the Honors community, and Honors events; and Tom, a freshman who is interested in creating a road map for his next four years in the LSA Honors Program.

After completing our personas, we moved on to comparative analysis - looking at the Michigan State Honors Program (Direct Comparison), the Michigan Community Scholars Program (Indirect Comparison), the Sweetland Writing Center (Parallel Comparison), the University of Michigan School of Information (Parallel Comparison), and the Michigan Secretary of State (Analogous Comparison). Examining these websites gave us some ideas to for improvements to the LSA Honors website. We also completed a Heuristic Analysis, which showed a few minor, but no major, heuristic issues.

In order to hear from a greater range of people, we also created a survey for current LSA Honors Students. The results of our survey supported the findings of our interviews - studnets think that the LSA honors website can be difficult to navigate when searching for information. Our usabilitiy tests, conducted with a mix of current and non-LSA Honors students, showed similar results.

Recommendations

Based on the results of our research, we came up with a few key recommendations. First, we suggested that the terminology used on the website - like page titles - should give users and idea about what information they can expect to find on each page. We also suggested that the navigation should group similar items together, in order to make it easier to find. Finally, adding more media to pages when possible would help people enjoy using the LSA Honors website more. These small changes have the potential to make the LSA Honors website much easier to navigate and parse through, and are two changes that the Honors Program has the capability to change within the AEM system.
