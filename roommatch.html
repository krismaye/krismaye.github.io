About the Problem

As an incoming graduate student at the University of Michigan, it can be difficult to find housing near campus. Because many graduate students are coming from places outside the state of Michigan, or even outside the United States, they may not be able come to Ann Arbor to visit potential apartments, and may have to conduct their housing search from afar. This can make it more difficult to find a suitable living arrangement. Additionally, graduate students may be interested in sharing housing with one or more roommates to cut down on costs, but may not know any other students with whom they could live. Even for returning students who may be looking to move to a new apartment, the process can be difficult because there is no centralized place to look for apartment listings. These factors can make the housing search stressful for graduate students.

Research & Feature Definition

In interviewing several of my peers about their housing searches, I learned that most of them felt the process was overly complicated. One person in particular said that she ended up moving into a house with other people she didn't know, because she felt overwhelmed with the thought of trying to find the perfect roommate. My interviewees also noted that they thought their housing searches would have been easier if there had been one place they could have found all of the information they needed; and that during their searches, they wanted to have more information about apartments (especially pictures or videos). Another interviewee also noted that she was intimidated by talking to landlords on the phone, and wanted to be able to communicate with them via email. Based on these interviews, I decided that some key features of my solution should be: providing consolidated information about all types of housing options (both on- and off-campus), providing details about each property (especially pictures/video when possible), allowing users to directly email landlords from within the system, and allowing users to match with people to find their "perfect" roommate(s).

After solidifying these features, I brainstormed with some other classmates about what shape my solution could take. In creating a matching system for roommates, we determined that it would be helpful to focus on quantitative aspects (such as "I wake up early", or "I like my apartment to be quiet"), since this would make it easier for people to quickly understand the content of other users' profiles. We also talked about how this app could differentiate itself from other solutions on the market (like Craigslist, or individual property managmement company websites). Although they are very fragmented, there are currently several solutions for finding housing in Ann Arbor, but none for finding roommates. Because of this, I decided that the differentiating feature of my app should be that it focuses on roommates first, and then housing.

Prototypes

Having decided that the focus of my app would be finding roommates, I looked at some possible user flows:

  • In the first scenario, users would see a housing map first, from which they could decide which apartment they were most interested in. From there, they would be able to search for the appropriate amount of roommates based on the apartment they selected.
  • In the second scenario, I had users match with roommates first, before moving on to the map of apartment listings.

Because the second scenario was more focused on roommate finding, I chose to use that workflow for my app. At this point, I began to flesh out more of the details. Early on, I wondered if my app should allow users to access some app content without having to create an account. However, I decided to create more privacy by requiring users to create an account before using any features of the app. Additionally, since the matching portion of the app would work by matching people based on their individual characteristics, having an account was essential, as it allowed users to fill out and save their characteristics and preferences.

With the features and workflow through my app determined, I created the first iteration of my paper prototype. Each of the screens took the user through the process of:

  1. creating their account,
  2. viewing their matches,
  3. sending roommate requests, and
  4. finding apartments.

After testing my first paper prototype with some of my classmates, I realized that there were some things that, while seeming intuitive to me, were not intuitive to others. For example, on the roommate detail page, I had nested two features, "favorite" and "send roommate request", within a single heart-shaped button; my tester told me that she didn't want to press it, because she wasn't sure what would happen. So, in my second prototype, I separated the two buttons and gave them labels to ensure that each button's function would be clear. Another thing that came up when testing my first prototype was that while people might be quiet, or messy, they might not necessarily care whether their roommate had similar characteristics (especially for the "night owl" vs. "early bird" characteristic). Because of this, I decided to add a toggle under each characteristic where the user could pick whether or not it was important that their roommates shared this quality with them. I also realized that I had built my paper prototype to work only if the user followed my intended workflow, and hadn't included a menu to let them move around to different screens on their own; this important feature was added in later iterations.

Paper prototype of my app

After improving my initial paper prototype, I moved on to a digital prototype. Using Adobe XD, I created each of my screens and linked them together using interactions. To use my app, users first create an account where they can fill out characteristics about themselves, like whether they are neat or messy, loud or quiet, etc. They can also indicate how important each of these characteristics are to them. After filling out their profile, users are taken to a screen with a list of matches ordered by how close they are to the user's preferences. From this list, they can view the profiles of any of their matches and either add the match to their favorites list (to save it for later), or send a roommate request to the match. After looking at roommates, users can view a screen with a map of apartment listings. When they first visit the map, they can see all apartment listings, but there are a variety of filters (price, number of bedrooms, neighborhood, etc.) that can be used to filter the results to better meet the needs of each user. Clicking on any of the pins on the map opens a new screen with the detail for the individual listing, where the user can add an apartment to their favorites list, which is later shared with anyone who accepts their roommate request. From the favorites list, users can contact landlords for any of their favorite apartments by email or phone.
